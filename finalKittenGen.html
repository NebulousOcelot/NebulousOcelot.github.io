Sex-linked Orange

The allele which causes orange-pigmented fur is sex-linked, meaning that it is carried on the X-chromosome. Male cats, having only one X-chromosome, can be either Black(Xo) or Orange(XO).

Female cats, however, have two X-chromosomes. A female cat with only one orange-carrying X-chromosome will be a tortoiseshell, with patches of black and orange pigment. This is also why female orange cats are considered rare.

Orange pigment is called Phaeomelanin, while black pigment is called Eumelanin.

The orange coat is also sometimes called "red." This is one of many language holdovers from the word "orange" being relatively new to the English language (ex: red foxes, red-tailed hawks, etc.).

Browning (Chocolate and Cinnamon)

Two recessive genes cause black pigment to be lightened and browned. Black(B) is dominant over Chocolate(b), which is dominant over Cinnamon(b1). Both forms of brown are rare in the wild.

Orange pigment is not affected by this gene.

Albinism & Colorpoints

The colorpoint coat, celebrated as the Siamese breed, is a form of albinism. Kittens are born white, their coats darkening as they age. The colder areas of the cat's body, most notably the extremities or "points", develop more pigment. Colorpoints in colder regions have darker coats.

Black colorpoints are often referred to as Seal Point.

A tabby colorpoint is called a Lynx Point. They have shadows of tabby stripes in their lighter fur.

The solid coat, C, is dominant over everything else. cs and cb are co-dominant. cs is the classic Siamese colorpoint, while cb is the darker Sepia colorpoint seen in the Burmese breed. When a cat has both cs and cb alleles, its coat is an even halfway point between the two; this is called Mink and is characteristic of the Tonkinese breed.

c2, the blue-eyed albino, is recessive to colorpoint but dominant to c, which is the pink-eyed albino.

Dilution (Gray)

The recessive Dilution gene dilutes both black and orange pigment. Black becomes Gray/"Blue", Chocolate becomes the rosy-gray Lilac, and Cinnamon becomes the light brown Fawn. Orange becomes Cream, also called Buff.

The dilution affects all of the pigment in a tortoiseshell's coat, producing Blue-Cream, Lilac-Cream, or Fawn-Cream.

Dilute Modifier (Caramelization)

The dominant Dilute Modifier gene affects already-diluted pigment, turning it a browner shade. This is referred to as caramelization. Gray becomes Caramel, Lilac becomes Taupe, and Cream becomes Apricot. (A Fawn-based caramel is certainly possible, but undoubtedly rare.)

Dominant White

The dominant white gene turns a cat's coat completely and totally white.

Blue-eyed white cats with this gene are often born deaf, as this gene can inhibit the development of the inner ear.

White Spotting (Bicolor)

The white spotting gene creates patches of white, non-pigmented fur on a cat. It is partially dominant over the solid coat.

Low-grade white spotting starts on the cat's underside, while mid-grade ("bicolor") advances onto the paws and face. High-grade white spotting leaves the ears and tail as the last splotches of color. It can sometimes be indistinguishable from dominant white.

Bicolor tortoiseshells are referred to as "calico".

All white or bicolor cats can have blue eyes, or more commonly, one blue eye and one with a normal color (odd eyes).

A cat without this gene can still have tiny white patches, most often on the underside. This is especially common in black cats.

Agouti (Tabby)

The tabby is the natural wild type of cats, and is dominant over the completely solid coat.

However, orange-pigmented fur will always be tabby, even if the cat is genetically not a tabby. This is called a pseudo- or ghost-tabby.

Tabbies are characterized by banded hair shafts, with bands of lightened pigment, alongside stripes of solid-colored hairs. A tabby's genetic color is the color of their stripes. Most "brown" tabbies have black stripes and are therefore genetically black; a chocolate or cinnamon tabby will have brown stripes.

The pattern of the tabby is controlled by other genes, but an "M" on the forehead and stripes on the legs and tail are near-universal. Marbled and rosetted tabbies have wildcat ancestry (as seen in the Bengal breed).

Ticked tabby overrides Spotted tabby which overrides the primary tabby type.

Ticked Tabby

The Ticked or Agouti Tabby is a special variant of tabby. It is essentially a tabby cat with the banded hair shafts, but no stripes, except maybe on the legs or tail. An unbroken coat of banded hairs.

Spotted Tabby

A spotted tabby's coat is spotted, instead of striped. There are intermediate stages between spotted and mackerel (striped) tabbies. It is possible for a "broken mackerel" tabby's broken stripes to resemble spots.

Classic & Mackerel Tabbies

The dominant mackerel tabby is the iconic, pin-stripey straight stripes pattern. The recessive classic tabby has large, blotched, swirling stripes, often forming a "bullseye" on the cat's side.

Shorthair and Longhair

The recessive longhair gene gives a cat longer fur. Separate genes control how silky or woolly the fur can be. Genes causing curly hair or hairlessness (ex: the Sphynx breed) are often found and made into specialized breeds.
